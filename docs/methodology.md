# Methodology

## Evidence hierarchy

Use sources in this order whenever possible:

1. Enacted law, bill text, amendment, executive order, regulation, court opinion, contract, procurement record, meeting agenda, or official dataset
2. Official agency, court, legislative, or local-government explanation
3. Direct statements from named officials, contractors, or organizations
4. Established civil-rights, civil-liberties, academic, or technical analysis
5. Reputable news reporting
6. Social-media posts and screenshots only as claims requiring verification

## Required distinctions

Every entry must separate:

- What the source literally says
- What officials claim it means
- What critics or supporters claim it means
- What the tracker concludes
- What cannot yet be determined

## Confidence labels

### High
Supported directly by primary documentation with little material ambiguity.

### Medium
Core facts are supported, but implementation, scope, or effect remains uncertain.

### Low
The item is based on incomplete records, conflicting accounts, or preliminary information.

Low-confidence material should not be framed as settled.

## Update triggers

Review an entry when:

- A bill is amended, passed, signed, vetoed, or withdrawn
- An agency publishes implementing guidance
- A court issues a ruling
- A contract or public record becomes available
- A factual correction is received
- Six months pass without review on an active issue

## Archiving

Where lawful and technically feasible, preserve:

- Original URL
- Retrieval date
- File name
- Cryptographic hash
- Archived URL
- Notes about later changes

Do not republish sensitive information merely because it appeared in a public record.
