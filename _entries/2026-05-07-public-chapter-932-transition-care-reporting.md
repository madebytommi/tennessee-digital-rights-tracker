## What happened?

Governor Bill Lee signed House Bill 754, the companion to Senate Bill 676, on May 7, 2026. It became Public Chapter 932 and took effect upon becoming law.

The law creates several related requirements:

1. Licensed Tennessee healthcare entities that provide or prescribe a statutorily defined “gender transition procedure” must report specified statistics to the Tennessee Department of Health.
2. Insurers covering statutorily defined detransition procedures must report specified claims statistics to the Department of Health.
3. Certain insurance policies that cover gender transition procedures must also cover detransition procedures, subject to different rules for marketplace and non-marketplace plans.
4. A gender clinic using state funds for gender transition procedures must agree to provide or pay for detransition procedures as a condition of receiving those funds.
5. Counties and municipalities may not prohibit specified mental-health therapy addressing an inconsistency between a minor’s sex and perceived gender or perceived sex.

This tracker entry concentrates on the reporting and privacy provisions.

## What the primary source says

### Scope and definitions

The law defines a “gender clinic” as a healthcare entity licensed under Tennessee health law that provides or prescribes a “gender transition procedure.” The enacted definition of “gender transition procedure” is written as a pharmaceutical or surgical intervention addressing an inconsistency involving a **minor’s** sex and perceived gender or perceived sex.

Because that definition is framed around minors, this entry does not treat Public Chapter 932 as a general reporting mandate for adult transition care. Tennessee already separately prohibits most transition-related medical procedures for minors under Tennessee Code § 68-33-103, which creates a significant question about when the new reporting provision will produce reportable records.

The final law does **not** include healthcare entities that only make referrals, and referral information was removed from the reporting requirements before enactment.

### Clinic reporting requirements

A covered gender clinic must submit statistics regarding each covered procedure on a Department of Health form. The required fields include:

| Required field | What the law requires |
|---|---|
| Procedure date | Date on which the covered procedure was prescribed |
| Patient demographics | Age and sex |
| Medication details | Drug name, dosage, frequency, duration, and administration method |
| Surgical details | Procedure type identified by CPT code |
| Geography | State of residence; county was removed by the final Senate amendment |
| Provider information | Name, contact information, and medical specialty of the prescribing healthcare professional |
| Diagnoses | Neurological, behavioral, or mental-health diagnoses received by the patient |

The form must be signed by the prescribing healthcare professional and transmitted no later than 15 days after the end of the calendar month in which the procedure was prescribed.

The law says these forms must not contain “individually identifiable health information” as defined by 45 CFR § 160.103. That federal definition covers health information that identifies a person or for which there is a reasonable basis to believe it can identify the person.

### Public reporting

The Department of Health must submit an annual statistical report by December 31, 2026, and annually afterward.

The version sent to the Senate speaker, House speaker, and legislative librarian must aggregate the data and must not identify individual gender clinics.

The version placed on the Department of Health website must:

- be available in a downloadable format;
- contain data that are “not aggregated”; and
- not identify individual providers.

The law does not define the exact public file format, row structure, date precision, suppression rules, or de-identification process.

### Enforcement

A healthcare professional who fails to comply is deemed to have engaged in unprofessional conduct. The statute requires disciplinary action to include a suspension of at least six months. It also provides for a civil fine of up to $150,000 against the gender clinic where the professional is employed.

The final Senate amendment narrowed the attorney general’s law-specific authority. The enacted language authorizes the attorney general to investigate a potential violation, but removed the bill’s earlier express authority to use a civil investigative demand or bring an enforcement action under these sections.

### Insurance reporting

An entity providing coverage for detransition procedures must report statistics including:

- number of claims;
- age and sex of the person receiving the procedure;
- if known, the date the person initially began a gender transition procedure; and
- state of residence.

County of residence was removed before enactment. These reporting forms also must not contain individually identifiable health information.

The statute requires submission within 15 days after the end of the relevant calendar month. The timing sentence refers to a month in which a claim for a “gender transition procedure” was approved, even though the surrounding subsection concerns detransition claims. The practical interpretation of that wording may require agency guidance or legal analysis.

## What officials say

The legislation labels its relevant sections “Right to Effective Care” and “Right to Public Transparency.” Legislative materials present the measure as increasing transparency and requiring insurers or state-supported clinics that cover or provide transition care to also support detransition care.

During Senate debate, sponsor Senator Brent Taylor said he believed the healthcare industry did not adequately assist people seeking to return to their birth gender. He also supported the amendment removing county-level information after privacy concerns were raised about people in smaller communities potentially being identifiable.

These statements describe the sponsors’ rationale; they do not resolve how the Department of Health will technically de-identify and publish the data.

## What advocates and critics say

The ACLU of Tennessee urged a veto and described the bill as a transgender healthcare registry. It argued that the combination of detailed health information, public reporting, professional suspension, and large fines could threaten privacy and discourage providers from treating transgender patients.

Tennessee Equality Project described the measure as trans-health data surveillance and argued that the amendments did not remove the danger created by extensive data collection. Tennessee Advocates for Planned Parenthood similarly argued that the reporting mandate intrudes on patient-provider confidentiality and increases stigma.

This tracker does **not** adopt “registry” as a neutral description of the law. The enacted statute expressly prohibits individually identifiable health information and does not authorize publication of patient names. The critics’ label reflects their argument that detailed, record-level reporting could still enable surveillance or re-identification depending on implementation.

## What is confirmed?

- Public Chapter 932 was signed on May 7, 2026, and became effective immediately.
- Its reporting definition is written around transition-related pharmaceutical or surgical interventions involving minors.
- Covered clinics must report detailed procedure, medication, demographic, provider, and diagnosis information to the Department of Health.
- County of residence was removed from both clinic and insurer reporting requirements before enactment.
- Submitted forms must not contain individually identifiable health information as defined in federal regulation.
- The public website report must not identify providers.
- The public website data must be downloadable and “not aggregated.”
- The first annual report is due by December 31, 2026.
- Professional noncompliance can trigger a suspension of at least six months, and the clinic may face a fine of up to $150,000.
- The final law authorizes the attorney general to investigate potential violations but removed the bill’s earlier express civil-demand and enforcement language.

## What remains uncertain?

As of the last review date, this project did not locate a publicly posted Department of Health reporting form, data dictionary, implementation manual, or public-report specification. The following issues therefore remain unresolved:

- Whether the Department will publish one row per procedure, claim, patient, clinic, or another unit of analysis
- Whether exact procedure dates will appear publicly or be generalized
- Whether age will be exact or grouped
- How rare medications, dosages, procedures, diagnoses, and demographic combinations will be handled
- Whether small-cell suppression or another statistical disclosure-control method will be used
- Which HIPAA de-identification method, if any, will guide the Department’s process
- How long submitted and published data will be retained
- Which state employees, contractors, or agencies may access the submitted provider-level information
- What audit logging, encryption, role-based access, incident-response, and breach-notification controls will apply
- Whether submitted forms or underlying datasets are exempt from Tennessee public-records disclosure
- How the reporting obligation operates alongside Tennessee’s existing prohibition on most transition-related procedures for minors
- How the apparent wording inconsistency in the insurance-reporting deadline will be interpreted
- Whether the Department of Commerce and Insurance has submitted, or CMS has approved, the required essential-health-benefits waiver; the statute’s 90-day application period had not yet expired on the last review date
- Whether litigation will challenge any portion of the law

The absence of public documentation does not prove that no internal policies or controls exist.

## Who may be affected?

The people most directly affected are:

- healthcare professionals and licensed entities that fall within the statutory definition of a gender clinic;
- minors whose care falls within the law’s definition, if any such procedures are lawfully provided or otherwise reported;
- insurers administering covered detransition benefits;
- transgender patients and families concerned about secondary use or publication of health information; and
- people seeking detransition-related care who may gain insurance coverage under parts of the law.

For adult transgender Tennesseans, the law may create concern because of its subject matter and the public-data requirement. However, the reporting definition examined here is framed around minors. This entry does not find a clear textual basis for claiming that an adult’s ordinary transition-related prescription must be reported under Public Chapter 932.

## Privacy and civil-liberties significance

The law contains real privacy protections: it removes county-level reporting, bars provider identification in the public report, and prohibits individually identifiable health information.

At the same time, it requires a public, non-aggregated dataset containing some combination of dates, age, sex, state, treatment details, and diagnoses. HHS guidance explains that properly de-identified health data can still retain a small, nonzero risk of re-identification. The level of risk depends on the values released, the availability of outside information, and the technical methods used to limit uniqueness and linkage.

The most defensible current conclusion is therefore:

> Public Chapter 932 does not authorize a named public patient list, but its unusual combination of detailed collection and non-aggregated public reporting creates legitimate privacy questions that cannot be resolved until Tennessee publishes the form, data structure, de-identification method, access rules, and security controls.

Whether the system is adequately protective is a technical and legal question requiring implementation evidence and, ideally, independent expert review.

## Lawful actions and resources

- Read Public Chapter 932 and the adopted amendments rather than relying only on headlines.
- Ask the Tennessee Department of Health to publish the reporting form, data dictionary, de-identification methodology, small-cell policy, retention schedule, access-control policy, and public-report specification.
- Request non-patient-level implementation records under the Tennessee Public Records Act, such as policies, contracts, security standards, and form-development documents. Do not request or redistribute identifiable medical information.
- Monitor the Department of Health website for the first report due by December 31, 2026.
- Ask the Department of Commerce and Insurance for the status of the required CMS waiver application after the statutory deadline.
