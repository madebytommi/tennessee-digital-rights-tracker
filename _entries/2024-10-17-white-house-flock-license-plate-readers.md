The absence of a public document does not prove that no policy, safeguard, audit, or restriction exists. Unknowns are identified rather than filled with assumptions.

## What happened?

The City of White House developed a local automated license plate reader, or ALPR, program using Flock Safety technology.

An August 2024 city agenda packet described a proposed leasing agreement with Flock Safety for four license plate readers. On October 17, 2024, the Board of Mayor and Aldermen adopted Resolution 24-12, authorizing the city to complete applications for licenses to install and operate law-enforcement ALPR cameras on Tennessee state highway rights-of-way.

The Electronic Frontier Foundation’s Atlas of Surveillance, relying on a public record produced by the Pittsboro Police Department in Indiana, reports that the White House Police Department operated four Flock Safety readers as of September 2025.

The underlying interagency record also listed White House Police Department’s four cameras in a shared-device list available to Pittsboro. For that relationship, the record showed:

- search access enabled;
- device-management access disabled; and
- the hotlist tool enabled.

This establishes at least one documented instance of an outside agency having search access to White House camera data as of September 11, 2025. It does not reveal White House’s complete current sharing list, whether the access was reciprocal, how often the access was used, or whether the configuration remains active.

## What the primary source says

### What ALPR systems collect

Tennessee Code § 55-10-302 defines an ALPR system as fixed high-speed cameras combined with computer algorithms that convert license plate images into computer-readable data.

The statute defines “captured plate data” broadly to include:

- GPS coordinates;
- date and time;
- a photograph;
- the license plate number; and
- other data captured by or derived from the ALPR system.

Flock Safety states that its cameras can also classify objective vehicle characteristics. Vendor capabilities and an individual agency’s enabled features are not necessarily identical, so this entry does not assume every Flock function is active in White House.

### White House’s state-right-of-way authorization

Resolution 24-12 authorizes the city to apply for state licenses for ALPR cameras located on state highway rights-of-way. The attached Tennessee Department of Transportation application states that such cameras may be permitted to aid criminal investigations or searches for missing or endangered people.

The TDOT process requires local legislative approval and review involving the Tennessee Department of Safety and Homeland Security. The application materials require a written agency ALPR policy that addresses data privacy, retention, and audit logs.

The state application also says that:

- approval is specific to the camera brand, model, and approved locations;
- relocation or equipment substitution requires a new application;
- TDOT may revoke permission;
- data management must occur in the United States; and
- the manufacturer or data custodian may not commercially use or sell captured plate data, apart from law-enforcement sharing authorized by law.

Resolution 24-12 proves that White House authorized the application process. This review did not locate a publicly posted final TDOT license showing which applications were approved or the current inventory of state-right-of-way cameras.

### Tennessee retention law

Tennessee Code § 55-10-302 generally prohibits a governmental entity from storing captured ALPR data for more than 90 days.

The law permits longer retention when data are part of an ongoing investigation. In that situation, the data must be destroyed at the conclusion of an investigation that produces no criminal charges, or at the conclusion of the related criminal action.

The 90-day period is a maximum, not necessarily White House’s actual setting. Flock Safety states that its default retention period is 30 days, after which data are hard-deleted, unless law or a customer agreement requires something different. Publicly available records reviewed for this entry do not establish whether White House uses 30 days, another period below 90 days, or investigative preservation procedures.

### Public-records confidentiality

Tennessee law treats captured plate data as confidential and not open for public inspection. Public Chapter 672, signed on April 7, 2026, removed the former July 1, 2026 expiration date from that confidentiality provision.

That restriction applies to the captured plate data themselves. It does not necessarily make every contract, policy, camera inventory, sharing configuration, audit summary, training document, or aggregate usage statistic confidential. The applicability of particular public-records exemptions can depend on the record and should be evaluated by qualified counsel.

### Search access, logging, and sharing

Flock Safety states that:

- access is tied to individual approved accounts;
- searches must relate to a defined investigation;
- each search requires a reason;
- searches are automatically recorded with a user and timestamp;
- supervisors can review search activity; and
- the customer agency controls whether its data are shared with other agencies.

Those are current vendor statements about platform controls. They do not independently prove White House’s local policy, the frequency of supervisory review, the quality of entered search reasons, or how violations are handled.

The Pittsboro shared-device record is direct evidence that White House data were shared outside Tennessee with at least one agency as of September 2025. Because Flock says sharing is enabled by the customer, that record makes the department’s complete sharing configuration and oversight process an important public-accountability question.

### The unsettled warrant question

This review did not locate a Tennessee appellate or Sixth Circuit decision squarely deciding whether law enforcement must obtain a warrant before searching a networked ALPR database for historical vehicle-location information.

In June 2026, the United States Supreme Court held in *Chatrie v. United States* that police conducted a Fourth Amendment search when they obtained a person’s detailed Google Location History. The Court emphasized the revealing nature of retrospective location databases, but the case concerned cell-phone location information and did not decide the constitutional rules for ALPR systems.

Flock Safety argues that ALPR observations on public roads are constitutionally different from comprehensive cell-phone tracking. Privacy advocates argue that a sufficiently broad, networked ALPR database can reconstruct sensitive travel patterns. The effect of *Chatrie* on future ALPR cases is therefore an open legal question, not a settled conclusion.

Tennessee lawmakers considered the proposed Protecting Everyone from Excessive Police Surveillance, or PEEPS, Act in 2026. The proposal would have imposed additional warrant, deletion, auditing, reporting, and access requirements for historical location data. It did not pass: the House bill was taken off notice in March 2026, and the Senate companion did not advance to enactment.

## What officials say

White House’s resolution says compliance with the state licensing process serves the health, safety, and welfare of city residents.

The TDOT application describes the authorized purpose for state-right-of-way ALPR cameras as aiding criminal investigations and searches for missing or endangered people.

Flock Safety promotes the technology as a tool for locating stolen vehicles, identifying vehicles connected to crimes, generating real-time hotlist alerts, and conducting historical investigations. Flock also says the local customer owns the data, controls sharing, and can review logged user searches.

These statements explain the government and vendor rationale. Publicly available White House materials reviewed for this entry did not include local performance statistics showing the number of alerts, searches, stolen-vehicle recoveries, missing-person cases, arrests, false alerts, or cases in which ALPR evidence was material.

## What advocates and critics say

The Electronic Frontier Foundation describes ALPR as a form of mass surveillance because the cameras collect records about every passing vehicle, even though most drivers are not suspected of wrongdoing.

EFF argues that time-and-location records can become highly revealing when combined across many cameras or retained over time. A travel history may expose visits to healthcare facilities, religious institutions, protests, political events, workplaces, homes, or other sensitive locations.

Critics also raise concerns about:

- mistaken plate reads or hotlist errors leading to dangerous stops;
- use for investigations unrelated to the purpose presented when cameras were approved;
- broad interstate or federal sharing;
- searches lacking sufficient factual justification;
- inadequate review of audit logs; and
- limited public visibility into systems whose underlying plate data are confidential.

These are risks and policy concerns, not findings that White House personnel have misused the system. This review found no public evidence establishing misuse by White House Police Department employees.

## What is confirmed?

- White House adopted Resolution 24-12 on October 17, 2024, authorizing applications for law-enforcement ALPR cameras on state highway rights-of-way.
- The attached TDOT process requires local approval and a written ALPR policy addressing privacy, retention, and audit logs.
- A September 2025 public record identified four White House Police Department Flock Safety cameras.
- The same record showed Pittsboro Police Department had search access to the four-camera White House entry, with device management disabled and the hotlist tool enabled.
- Tennessee law defines captured plate data to include location, date, time, photographs, plate numbers, and derived data.
- Tennessee generally caps government retention at 90 days, with an exception for data preserved as part of an ongoing investigation.
- Captured plate data are confidential under Tennessee public-records law, and the former 2026 expiration date for that protection was removed.
- Flock states that its default retention period is 30 days and that searches are user-linked, reason-based, logged, and reviewable.
- The Supreme Court’s 2026 *Chatrie* decision addressed cell-phone location history, not ALPR databases.
- Tennessee’s proposed 2026 PEEPS Act did not become law.

## What remains uncertain?

As of the last review date, this project did not locate a publicly posted White House Police Department ALPR policy, current contract, full sharing report, audit report, or aggregate usage report. The following questions remain unresolved:

- Whether White House still operates exactly four Flock cameras
- Whether additional city-owned, privately owned, mobile, or partner cameras are searchable by the department
- Whether all cameras covered by Resolution 24-12 received final TDOT approval
- The current contract term, renewal status, total cost, and funding source
- White House’s actual retention setting and investigative-preservation procedure
- The complete list of agencies that can search White House data
- Whether statewide, nationwide, radius-based, one-to-one, or federal sharing is enabled
- The criteria and approval process for adding a plate to a local hotlist
- Whether an alert must be independently confirmed before a traffic stop or other enforcement action
- Which employees may search the system and what training they receive
- What information must be entered as the investigative reason for a search
- How frequently supervisors review query logs
- Whether the city conducts periodic independent audits
- Whether misuse has ever been alleged, investigated, or substantiated
- How false reads, incorrect hotlist entries, and disputed data are handled
- The number and type of searches, alerts, recoveries, arrests, and cases supported by the system
- Whether White House has written restrictions concerning immigration, reproductive healthcare, political activity, religious activity, or other sensitive investigations
- Whether private Flock camera owners share data with the department and under what conditions
- What cybersecurity, breach-notification, account-security, and incident-response controls apply locally
- How *Chatrie* or future federal and Tennessee decisions will affect warrant requirements for historical ALPR searches

The confidentiality of individual plate records should not prevent the city from publishing policies, sharing categories, aggregate statistics, audit findings, and other oversight information that does not expose a person’s movements or an active investigation.

## Who may be affected?

The system can affect any person traveling in or through White House in a vehicle visible to a participating camera, not only people suspected of crimes.

Potentially affected groups include:

- local residents and commuters;
- visitors and interstate travelers;
- registered vehicle owners and drivers who may not be the registered owner;
- passengers whose presence may be inferred from other evidence;
- people incorrectly associated with a hotlisted or misread plate;
- crime victims, missing people, and vehicle owners who may benefit from faster identification or recovery; and
- neighboring and out-of-state agencies that receive or contribute searchable data.

A plate observation does not by itself establish who was driving, why the vehicle was present, or whether any unlawful activity occurred.

## Privacy and civil-liberties significance

A license plate is displayed publicly, and a single observation on a public road is less revealing than continuous cell-phone tracking. The privacy significance changes when large numbers of observations become searchable across time and across jurisdictions.

The available evidence supports two conclusions at once:

1. ALPR technology can help investigators locate vehicles connected to crimes or endangered people.
2. A networked historical database can expose movements of people who are not suspected of wrongdoing and can reveal sensitive associations when access and sharing are too broad.

Tennessee’s 90-day limit and confidentiality rule are meaningful safeguards. However, confidentiality creates a transparency tradeoff: it protects drivers from public disclosure while also preventing residents from directly examining the raw dataset to assess accuracy, scale, and use. That makes public policies, aggregate statistics, access lists, and audit summaries especially important.

The documented Pittsboro access record shows that White House’s program was not isolated to searches by local personnel alone. It does not establish abuse, but it confirms that cross-jurisdiction sharing is a real operational feature rather than a purely hypothetical possibility.

The most defensible current conclusion is:

> White House has a documented Flock Safety ALPR program and has shared searchable camera access outside Tennessee. State law limits retention and protects raw plate data from public disclosure, but the public record is not yet sufficient to evaluate the department’s current sharing scope, search standards, audit rigor, error controls, effectiveness, or compliance in practice.

Resolving those questions requires local policy and audit records, not assumptions about either good faith or misconduct.

## Lawful actions and resources

- Read Resolution 24-12 and the attached TDOT application requirements.
- Request the current Flock contract, amendments, invoices, renewal records, funding source, and general camera inventory from the City of White House.
- Request the White House Police Department’s written ALPR policy, training requirements, retention schedule, hotlist rules, sharing policy, and audit procedure.
- Request the current list of agencies or sharing categories with access to White House data, while avoiding requests for individual drivers’ confidential plate histories.
- Request aggregate annual statistics for searches, alerts, confirmed matches, recoveries, arrests, false alerts, complaints, misuse investigations, and disciplinary findings.
- Request documentation of final TDOT licenses and any state security review for cameras on state highway rights-of-way.
- Ask the Board of Mayor and Aldermen to require periodic public reporting, policy review, and an independent audit before contract renewal or expansion.
- Ask that the public policy clearly define permissible purposes, prohibited sensitive uses, supervisory review, warrant procedures, error correction, and consequences for misuse.
- Submit records requests through the city’s Records Management process. The city identifies the police Records Clerk and City Recorder as public-records contacts.
- Do not publish identifiable plate histories, active investigative details, or information that could endanger a private person.
- For questions about constitutional rights, public-records exemptions, or an individual police encounter, consult a qualified Tennessee attorney.
