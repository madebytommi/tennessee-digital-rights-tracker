## What happened?

Governor Bill Lee signed House Bill 1665 on April 13, 2026. The bill was later assigned Public Chapter 748 and is scheduled to take effect on October 1, 2026.

The House passed the final amended bill 72-21 on March 16, 2026. The Senate passed the House bill 27-6 on March 19, 2026. The adopted House amendment replaced the bill's prior language and became the operative text.

The law creates two connected sets of restrictions:

1. A new insurance provision limiting when health insurers, managed care organizations, and other reimbursement entities may require or request specified gender-related questions.
2. A new healthcare-provider part limiting when covered providers may ask minors those questions and regulating consent, electronic forms, parental access, exceptions, and professional discipline.

The law is enacted but was not yet effective as of the last review date. The tracker therefore uses **Monitoring** status rather than treating implementation as complete.

## What the primary source says

### Questions covered by the law

The provider provision generally prohibits a covered healthcare provider from knowingly or willfully asking a minor, verbally or in writing:

- whether the minor feels normal in the minor's body;
- whether the minor believes the minor is the correct gender;
- whether the minor identifies as a gender different from the minor's sex; or
- a question intended to elicit statements about gender confusion or gender dysphoria.

The law is broader than electronic intake forms. It regulates both verbal and written questions, while also specifically addressing forms, tablets, and questionnaires.

### When a covered provider may ask

A covered healthcare provider may ask one of the listed questions when both of the following are satisfied:

- the parent is fully informed and gives written consent to the question; and
- the question directly relates to the diagnosis or treatment of a specific medical or psychological condition currently being evaluated.

The enacted text does not prescribe a standard consent form, explain how long consent lasts, state whether consent may cover multiple encounters, or describe how consent must be represented inside an EHR.

### Written and electronic intake tools

A covered healthcare provider may not include questions relating to gender confusion or gender dysphoria on written intake forms, electronic tablets, or questionnaires directed to a minor unless the consent and clinical-relevance requirements are satisfied.

This language is directly relevant to:

- pediatric and family-practice intake templates;
- electronic check-in tablets;
- pre-visit web questionnaires;
- EHR flowsheets and screening instruments;
- patient-portal questionnaires; and
- third-party digital intake systems used on behalf of a healthcare facility.

The law does not expressly state whether historical answers or previously configured fields must be deleted, hidden, retained, or migrated.

### Insurance, reimbursement, and quality scoring

The new insurance section prohibits a health insurance issuer, managed care organization, or entity providing reimbursement for healthcare services from requiring or requesting a provider or facility to ask the listed questions for any purpose, including as a condition of:

- payment;
- credentialing;
- quality scoring;
- compliance; or
- participation.

Those entities also may not deny or reduce payment, impose penalties, or otherwise disadvantage a provider for failing to ask the questions.

The final insurance section does not state a section-specific penalty within its own text. This entry does not infer what general insurance, administrative, contractual, or consumer-protection remedies might apply.

### Covered providers and excluded professionals

For the provider part, the law defines healthcare provider broadly to include licensed or regulated professionals, establishments, and facilities authorized to provide medical or mental-health services in Tennessee.

The definition excludes:

- psychologists;
- psychiatrists;
- licensed professional counselors;
- marriage and family therapists;
- licensed clinical pastoral therapists; and
- licensed clinical social workers.

Because the exclusion is built into the definition, those listed professionals are outside the provider restrictions in the new Title 63 part. This entry does not assume how the separate insurer provision will be interpreted for every professional or facility arrangement.

### Other exceptions and exclusions

The provider restriction does not apply to questions about a female minor's menstrual period.

A provider may ask follow-up questions when a minor first volunteers information related to one of the listed topics.

A provider may privately ask the listed questions when, using reasonable medical judgment, the provider reasonably believes a minor is trafficked or is a victim of brutality, abuse, or neglect and needs to conduct screening to decide whether a report is required.

The entire new provider part does not apply to a minor who is receiving medical treatment without parental consent under another Tennessee law.

For purposes of the law, the definition of minor also excludes certain people under eighteen, including an emancipated person, a person needing specified emergency treatment, a current or former member of the armed forces or a reserve or national guard unit, and a parent of a minor child who has full custody of that child.

### Parental access

The law states that a parent must have full access to all written forms, questionnaires, or electronic assessments presented to the parent's minor child in a healthcare setting.

The text does not expressly resolve:

- whether full access always includes the child's completed answers or only access to the instruments presented;
- whether access must be immediate or available on request;
- whether access must be provided through a patient portal;
- how proxy access, segmented records, confidential notes, and adolescent accounts must be configured;
- whether access extends to drafts, screening scores, metadata, or audit trails; or
- how the provision interacts in practice with federal privacy rules and Tennessee laws allowing some minors to receive care without parental consent.

Federal HIPAA guidance generally treats parents as personal representatives of unemancipated minors, but recognizes exceptions tied to state law, confidential care, and abuse, neglect, or endangerment. Public Chapter 748 itself also excludes treatment lawfully provided without parental consent from the new provider part.

### Enforcement

A covered provider's violation of the prohibited-question section constitutes unprofessional conduct subject to discipline by the provider's licensing board.

The law does not limit mandated reporting for child abuse or neglect, restrict specified emergency medical care, or prevent a minor from voluntarily reporting abuse, exploitation, or danger to self or others.

The act contains a severability clause.

## What officials say

The General Assembly included findings declaring that parents have the right to make physical and mental healthcare decisions for their minor children and should remain fully informed. The findings state that minors are increasingly being asked gender-related questions without parental knowledge or consent, that some providers report insurer or third-party pressure to collect the information, and that the state has an interest in preventing solicitation of sensitive information unrelated to legitimate medical purposes.

Those are legislative findings and policy judgments, not independently established empirical conclusions by this tracker.

House sponsor Representative Aron Maberry told a House committee that he did not intend to interrupt patient-initiated conversations, but objected to clinicians introducing the topic when a child had not raised it. A Tennessee House Republican Caucus update presented the bill as a parental-consent and insurer-pressure measure.

The bill's official fiscal summary classified its fiscal impact as not significant.

As of the last review date, this project did not locate publicly posted implementation guidance specific to Public Chapter 748 from the Tennessee Department of Health, the relevant health-professional licensing boards, TennCare, the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance, or a major Tennessee hospital or insurer.

## What advocates and critics say

Organizations supporting the law frame it as a parental-rights protection and argue that healthcare providers or insurers should not introduce gender-related concepts to minors or require those questions for payment and quality programs without parental involvement and a specific clinical reason.

The Tennessee Equality Project and Human Rights Campaign describe Public Chapter 748 as medical censorship. Their joint report argues that the law may interfere with confidential preventive care, routine screening, clinical standards, insurer data collection, accreditation, quality assurance, and a minor's ability to disclose sensitive information in an unsupportive or unsafe home.

The American Academy of Pediatrics' 2026 confidentiality guidance is not a statement specifically about Public Chapter 748, but it provides relevant technical context. The AAP warns that electronic health records and patient portals can inadvertently disclose confidential adolescent information to parents or guardians and recommends deliberate privacy protections for sensitive data.

These competing positions identify real policy questions. They do not establish how every Tennessee provider will implement the law or how courts and licensing boards will interpret disputed language.

## What is confirmed?

- Governor Bill Lee signed HB 1665 on April 13, 2026.
- The bill became Public Chapter 748 and takes effect October 1, 2026.
- The final House vote was 72-21 and the final Senate vote was 27-6.
- The law regulates specified verbal and written gender-related questions asked of minors by covered healthcare providers.
- Written parental consent and direct relevance to a condition currently being evaluated are required before a covered provider asks the listed questions, unless an exception applies.
- The law specifically addresses written intake forms, electronic tablets, and questionnaires.
- Insurers and reimbursement entities may not require or request providers to ask the listed questions for payment, credentialing, quality scoring, compliance, or participation.
- The insurer provision also prohibits disadvantaging a provider for failing to ask the questions.
- The provider definition excludes specified licensed mental-health and pastoral professionals.
- The law includes exceptions or exclusions for volunteered information, trafficking and abuse screening, menstrual questions, emergency care, specified minors, and treatment legally received without parental consent.
- Parents must have full access to forms, questionnaires, and electronic assessments presented to their minor children in healthcare settings.
- A provider violation of the prohibited-question section may be treated as unprofessional conduct by the relevant licensing board.
- The law does not direct providers to delete historical EHR data or prescribe a patient-portal configuration.

## What remains uncertain?

As of the last review date, the following implementation questions remained unresolved in publicly available materials:

- Which licensing boards will issue guidance, policies, declaratory orders, FAQs, or disciplinary standards before October 1, 2026
- Whether the Department of Health will issue cross-board guidance for facilities and professionals
- How providers will document that a parent was fully informed
- Whether consent must identify each specific question
- Whether consent is encounter-specific, condition-specific, time-limited, or revocable
- How electronic consent signatures will be validated and retained
- How providers will determine that a question is directly related to the condition currently being evaluated
- Whether standardized pediatric screening tools will be revised, removed, segmented, or preceded by consent workflows
- Whether insurers will revise quality measures, credentialing instructions, reimbursement manuals, data dictionaries, or provider contracts
- Which existing insurer, accreditation, or quality programs actually require or request the questions prohibited by the law
- Whether EHR vendors will create Tennessee-specific templates, alerts, hard stops, suppression rules, or permission settings
- Whether removed fields and historical answers will remain in active records, archives, audit logs, interfaces, data warehouses, claims systems, or backups
- Whether parents' full access includes completed responses, scoring results, metadata, or only the forms and assessments presented
- How patient-portal proxy access will be configured for adolescents
- How providers will segment records for minors receiving care without parental consent under Tennessee law
- How abuse, neglect, trafficking, and endangerment exceptions will be documented without exposing a minor to additional risk
- How the law applies when a general medical provider collaborates with an excluded mental-health professional
- What enforcement process and penalties apply to insurers under the final insurance section
- Whether litigation will challenge the law before or after its effective date
- Whether implementation will reduce collection of unnecessary sensitive data, reduce clinically useful screening, or produce different effects across healthcare settings

## Who may be affected?

Potentially affected groups include:

- minors receiving care from covered Tennessee healthcare providers;
- transgender, nonbinary, gender-questioning, intersex, and other LGBTQ minors;
- minors whose gender-related concerns are unknown to or unsupported by a parent;
- parents and people with medical decision-making authority;
- pediatricians, family physicians, nurses, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, hospitals, clinics, and healthcare facilities;
- psychologists, psychiatrists, counselors, marriage and family therapists, clinical pastoral therapists, and clinical social workers whose statutory treatment differs from covered providers;
- health insurers, managed care organizations, TennCare contractors, accreditation programs, and entities that score or reimburse care;
- EHR, patient-portal, digital intake, clinical-decision-support, and quality-reporting vendors;
- compliance teams, privacy officers, medical-records staff, and health-information-management professionals; and
- Tennessee licensing boards responsible for investigating alleged unprofessional conduct.

The law can affect minors who do not identify as LGBTQ because the listed questions may appear in general pediatric screening and intake workflows.

## Privacy and civil-liberties significance

Public Chapter 748 creates a genuine privacy tradeoff.

Restricting routine collection may reduce the creation, storage, exchange, and secondary use of highly sensitive gender-related information. Preventing insurers from requiring those questions may also reduce pressure to collect information that is not necessary for a particular patient's treatment.

At the same time, requiring parental involvement and granting access to forms and electronic assessments may reduce confidential space for a minor to disclose identity, distress, family rejection, abuse, or safety concerns. The practical effect will depend heavily on how providers interpret the volunteer, abuse, confidential-treatment, and professional-exclusion provisions.

The law is especially relevant to digital rights because implementation will likely occur through technical systems rather than paper policy alone. Intake forms, EHR fields, patient portals, consent modules, proxy accounts, insurer data feeds, quality dashboards, backups, and audit logs will determine what information is asked, stored, displayed, shared, or suppressed.

The most defensible current conclusion is:

> Public Chapter 748 establishes clear restrictions on specified gender-related questions and insurer requirements, but it leaves healthcare organizations substantial technical and compliance work before October 1, 2026. The public record does not yet establish how Tennessee providers will redesign electronic intake, document consent, manage historical data, or reconcile parental access with confidential minor care.

## Lawful actions and resources

- Read Public Chapter 748 and the adopted House amendment rather than relying only on summaries of the introduced bill.
- Watch the Tennessee Department of Health and relevant licensing-board pages for implementation guidance before October 1, 2026.
- Healthcare organizations can inventory pediatric intake forms, EHR fields, tablet questionnaires, patient-portal forms, clinical alerts, consent workflows, insurer requirements, and proxy-access settings with qualified legal and compliance review.
- Parents and patients can ask a provider for its written policy on adolescent confidentiality, consent, portal access, and the handling of gender-related questions.
- Public-interest researchers can request non-patient implementation materials, including board guidance, hospital policies, blank consent forms, blank intake templates, training materials, insurer bulletins, and aggregate disciplinary information.
- Do not request or publish a minor's completed forms, portal messages, identity, diagnosis, treatment history, or other private medical information.
- Report factual or sourcing problems through the tracker's correction form.
- Consult qualified counsel or an appropriate healthcare professional for questions involving an individual patient's rights, safety, confidentiality, or treatment.
