## What happened?

Governor Bill Lee signed House Bill 2498 on April 16, 2026. It was assigned Public Chapter 765 on April 24 and took effect July 1, 2026.

The House passed the bill 73-14 on March 12, 2026. The Senate passed the House bill 27-6 on March 30. The official legislative history lists no amendments, so the introduced bill text became the enacted text.

Public Chapter 765 adds a statutory restriction to Tennessee's Medicaid program. It directs the Division of TennCare not to provide coverage or reimbursement for specified medical procedures when their purpose is to enable an individual to identify with or live as an identity the statute describes as inconsistent with the individual's sex, or to treat distress arising from a discordance between sex and asserted identity.

The operative prohibition refers to an **individual**, not only to a minor. The law's definition of puberty blocker refers to use in a minor, but the surgical and hormone provisions are not written with an overall age limit.

The tracker uses **Active** status because the prohibition is in effect. Confidence is **Medium** because the statutory terms are documented, while the public record reviewed does not fully establish how TennCare and each contractor are processing affected claims, prescriptions, notices, certifications, and appeals.

## What the primary source says

### Procedures covered by the statutory definition

For purposes of the new TennCare restriction, a medical procedure means:

- surgically removing, modifying, altering, or entering tissues, cavities, or organs of a human being; or
- prescribing, administering, or dispensing a puberty blocker, androgen, or estrogen.

The statute defines a puberty blocker as a drug or device used to suppress hormone production in a minor in order to stop, delay, or suppress pubertal development. It defines hormone as androgen or estrogen.

The law does not expressly name psychotherapy, counseling, social transition support, voice therapy, laboratory testing, or every health service associated with gender dysphoria. This entry does not treat those services as prohibited unless a stronger source establishes that result.

### The restriction is purpose-based

The same surgery or medication may be treated differently depending on why it is provided.

TennCare remains permitted to cover or reimburse a procedure used to treat:

- a congenital defect;
- precocious puberty;
- disease; or
- physical injury.

For this exception, the statute says disease does not include gender dysphoria, gender identity disorder, gender incongruence, or any mental condition, disorder, disability, or abnormality.

Because hormones and surgeries can have many clinical uses, implementation will likely require claims, pharmacy, prior-authorization, diagnosis, and medical-necessity systems to distinguish the purpose of treatment. The enacted text does not publish a code list or specify the exact electronic logic contractors must use.

### Telehealth

The prohibition applies to procedures performed or administered in Tennessee and to procedures performed or administered on an individual located in Tennessee, including through telehealth.

The text therefore reaches covered prescribing or treatment delivered remotely to a TennCare member located in the state. It does not explain how location will be verified, which telehealth claim fields will be used, or how cross-state providers and pharmacies will receive notice.

### Temporary continuity provision

A procedure that began before July 1, 2026 may remain eligible for coverage or reimbursement when:

- the procedure concludes on or before March 31, 2027; and
- the individual's treating physician certifies in writing that, in the physician's good-faith medical judgment and based on the facts known at the time, ending the procedure would be harmful to the individual.

The certification must:

- include the findings supporting the certification; and
- become part of the individual's medical record.

The continuity exception does not authorize coverage for a different medical procedure when the sole purpose of that later procedure falls within the statutory prohibition.

The law does not define in operational detail what counts as the same procedure. Publicly available materials reviewed for this entry did not resolve whether changes in medication dose, formulation, route, manufacturer, dispensing pharmacy, prescriber, or administration schedule remain the same procedure.

### Medical-record documentation

The certification and supporting findings become protected clinical documentation within the patient's medical record. The statute does not create a public registry or direct public disclosure of the certification.

It also does not specify:

- a standard certification form;
- which plan or pharmacy personnel may request or view it;
- whether the full supporting findings or only an attestation must be sent with a claim or authorization request;
- whether the certification must be renewed;
- how it must be transmitted;
- how long it must be retained beyond ordinary medical-record requirements;
- whether it will be stored in an EHR, utilization-management platform, document repository, claims attachment system, or multiple systems; or
- what audit, access-control, or secondary-use restrictions apply.

### Prior coverage practice

Public Chapter 765 creates a statutory prohibition. One post-session news report stated that TennCare already did not cover gender-affirming care before the law, suggesting that the act may codify or reinforce an existing administrative exclusion rather than terminate a uniformly available benefit.

The public sources reviewed did not establish the exact pre-July 2026 coverage history for every affected drug, surgery, diagnosis, plan, age group, medical circumstance, or appeal. This entry therefore does not claim that every listed service was routinely payable immediately before the law took effect.

## TennCare's operational structure

TennCare is an integrated managed-care Medicaid program. TennCare states that medical, behavioral-health, and long-term services are delivered through at-risk managed care organizations, while prescription coverage is handled through a separate Pharmacy Benefits Administrator.

Public TennCare materials identify BlueCare Tennessee, UnitedHealthcare Community Plan, and Wellpoint provider portals in the claims and appeals process. Public member materials also identify OptumRx as the pharmacy benefit administrator.

That structure means implementation may occur across several systems rather than in one TennCare database, including:

- managed-care medical claims systems;
- pharmacy point-of-sale systems;
- prior-authorization platforms;
- provider portals;
- EHR and document-upload workflows;
- telehealth claims and location fields;
- member notice systems;
- provider reconsideration systems;
- member appeal systems; and
- state oversight and reporting systems.

This is an operational inference from the program structure, not proof of the specific configuration used for Public Chapter 765.

### Public implementation materials located

UnitedHealthcare's Tennessee Medicaid policy library published a July 2026 update bulletin listing changes effective July 1 to policies addressing gonadotropin-releasing-hormone analogs and testosterone replacement or supplementation therapy.

The bulletin does not identify Public Chapter 765, does not describe a transition-related exclusion, and expressly states that listing a service does not itself imply coverage. The Tennessee policy library also did not display a policy titled Gender Dysphoria Treatment when searched during this review.

Those July 1 updates are a relevant implementation lead, but the public record reviewed does not establish whether they were caused by Public Chapter 765 or how they affect a particular TennCare member.

As of the last review date, this project did not locate a clearly labeled Public Chapter 765 implementation bulletin, coverage manual section, physician certification form, member notice, claims-edit notice, or pharmacy notice from TennCare, BlueCare, UnitedHealthcare Community Plan of Tennessee, Wellpoint, or OptumRx.

## Claims, denials, and appeals

TennCare's provider guidance says a provider who disagrees with a claim outcome may submit an appeal or reconsideration to the applicable managed care organization. If dissatisfied with that result, the provider may file a complaint with TennCare Oversight at the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance.

The enacted law does not itself prescribe:

- a member-facing denial reason;
- a provider remittance code;
- a pharmacy rejection code;
- a special appeal form;
- an expedited review standard;
- an evidentiary standard for the physician certification;
- a process for challenging whether a treatment is for a prohibited purpose;
- a process for resolving whether a changed treatment is the same procedure; or
- publication of aggregate denial, authorization, continuity, or appeal statistics.

Existing TennCare and contractor appeal procedures may apply, but this entry does not assume that they answer every issue created by the new law.

## What officials say

The Tennessee Senate Republican Caucus described Public Chapter 765 as a taxpayer-stewardship measure intended to keep TennCare focused on physical health and medical necessity. Its July 1 enactment summary emphasized that treatment for congenital conditions, early puberty, disease, and physical injury remains eligible for coverage.

Those statements describe the sponsors' and caucus's policy rationale. They are not independent findings by this tracker about medical effectiveness, fiscal impact, or the experiences of individual patients.

The official fiscal summary classified the law's fiscal effect as not significant.

## What advocates and critics say

The Tennessee Equality Project opposed HB 2498 and described it as a ban on TennCare coverage for gender-affirming care. LGBTQ advocates generally argue that purpose-based exclusions deny transgender people coverage for treatments that may be covered when prescribed to other patients for different indications.

Clinical organizations have published standards supporting individualized assessment and hormone treatment for appropriately evaluated transgender patients. Those clinical materials are relevant context, but they do not determine TennCare's legal obligations under Public Chapter 765 or establish that every requested service must be covered.

A Nashville Scene session review reported that TennCare did not cover gender-affirming care before enactment. That report complicates claims about the law's immediate practical change but does not make the statute insignificant: the act places the exclusion in state law, specifies telehealth reach, and creates a time-limited certification process for existing procedures.

## What is confirmed?

- Governor Bill Lee signed HB 2498 on April 16, 2026.
- The bill became Public Chapter 765 and took effect July 1, 2026.
- The final House vote was 73-14 and the final Senate vote was 27-6.
- The official legislative history lists no amendments.
- The prohibition applies to an individual and is not written as a minors-only restriction.
- TennCare may not cover or reimburse specified surgeries, puberty blockers, androgen, or estrogen when provided for the transition-related purposes stated in the law.
- The prohibition applies to an individual located in Tennessee, including through telehealth.
- Procedures for congenital defects, precocious puberty, disease, or physical injury remain eligible under the stated exception.
- For that exception, disease excludes gender dysphoria, gender identity disorder, gender incongruence, and any mental condition, disorder, disability, or abnormality.
- A procedure begun before July 1 may qualify for temporary continuity through March 31, 2027.
- Continuity requires a treating physician's written good-faith certification that stopping would be harmful.
- The certification must contain supporting findings and become part of the medical record.
- The continuity provision does not cover a different procedure when its sole purpose falls within the statutory prohibition.
- The statute does not itself provide a code list, certification template, denial code, retention schedule, access rule, or aggregate reporting requirement.
- Public TennCare materials show that medical benefits and pharmacy benefits are administered through different managed-care components.

## Who may be affected?

Potentially affected groups include:

- transgender, nonbinary, and gender-diverse TennCare members of any age;
- TennCare members receiving hormones or procedures for non-transition-related indications who may be subject to additional documentation or review;
- people who began an affected procedure before July 1, 2026;
- treating physicians responsible for continuity certifications;
- surgeons, primary-care clinicians, endocrinologists, telehealth clinicians, pharmacies, and laboratories;
- BlueCare Tennessee, UnitedHealthcare Community Plan, Wellpoint, OptumRx, and their employees or subcontractors;
- hospital billing teams, pharmacy teams, prior-authorization staff, utilization reviewers, appeals staff, privacy officers, and health-information-management professionals;
- EHR, e-prescribing, claims-clearinghouse, telehealth, pharmacy, and utilization-management vendors; and
- advocates, attorneys, and public-interest researchers reviewing coverage denials and appeal outcomes.

## Privacy and civil-liberties significance

Public Chapter 765 is both a healthcare-access rule and a data-governance rule.

To enforce a purpose-based exclusion, TennCare and its contractors may need to evaluate diagnosis, treatment purpose, clinical notes, medication history, procedure history, telehealth location, prior claims, and physician certifications. That can increase the number of systems and personnel involved in processing sensitive information concerning gender identity and medical treatment.

The continuity provision requires a physician to place a written harm assessment and supporting findings into the medical record. That documentation may protect a patient from immediate interruption, but it also creates a durable record containing particularly sensitive clinical reasoning. The statute does not specify data minimization, segmentation, access logging, contractor retention, or deletion requirements for copies used outside the treating provider's record.

The law may also produce automated classification problems. A drug or procedure can be covered for one indication and prohibited for another, so simplistic code-only blocks could affect people receiving the same treatment for an allowed purpose. The public record does not establish whether contractors use diagnosis-aware review, manual exceptions, or safeguards against false denials.

The most defensible current conclusion is:

> Public Chapter 765 clearly places a purpose-based TennCare coverage prohibition into Tennessee law and creates a temporary, documentation-heavy continuity process. The public record does not yet show how TennCare and its contractors identify affected claims, protect certification records, distinguish allowed indications, communicate denials, or measure appeals and treatment interruptions.
